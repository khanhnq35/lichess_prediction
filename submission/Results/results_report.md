# Results Report

## 1. Run Overview

The final reported run used the no-Stockfish boosting profile:

```bash
python Solution.py --target-games 100000 --output-dir outputs_solution_improvements_100k_final --model-profile boosting
```

The run is fully reproducible from the public Lichess monthly database. It streams the `.pgn.zst` archive, filters the first eligible Blitz games, trains models on the first 80% of games, and evaluates on the last 20%.

## 5. Classification Interpretation

### Before Game

The before-game model performs almost exactly like the Elo expected-score baseline. This is expected because pre-game Elo difference is the strongest available causal signal. The learned logistic model slightly improves log loss and Brier score over the raw Elo formula.

### After 3 Full Moves

The after-3 model reaches ROC-AUC `0.578667`, which is similar to the before-game model. This is not a failure; 6 plies is still very early in most chess games. Many games remain in common opening structures, and decisive future blunders are not yet observable.

The value of the after-3 model is that it maintains a stable signal while adding early board-state awareness. It is a realistic lightweight baseline, not an engine-strength evaluator.

### After 10 Full Moves

The after-10 model is the strongest White-win classifier:

- ROC-AUC improves from `0.578805` before game to `0.622593` after 10 moves.
- Log loss improves from `0.678818` to `0.663965`.
- Brier score improves from `0.243280` to `0.236364`.
- Accuracy improves from `0.552550` to `0.579900`.

This indicates that the model gains useful signal from:

- Material and piece placement.
- Castling and development.
- Mobility and center control.
- Clock/time-pressure features within the first 20 plies.

## 8. Elo Regression Results

| Model | White MAE | White RMSE | White R2 | Black MAE | Black RMSE | Black R2 |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Elo after 10 | 29.241 | 82.072 | 0.950259 | 29.376 | 82.509 | 0.949865 |
| Mean baseline | 300.224 | 368.031 | -0.000222 | 300.586 | 368.529 | -0.000195 |

The model reduces average Elo error by roughly 90% relative to the mean baseline. This is the strongest part of the project.

The main reason is causal player history. If a player appears earlier in the same chronological stream, their prior observed Elo and prior opponent strength are highly predictive of current Elo. This is not leakage because the history is computed before updating with the current game. However, this performance should be interpreted as same-month stream performance, not necessarily cold-start performance for unseen players.

## 12. Practical Usefulness

The White-win models should be viewed as lightweight probabilistic ranking models. A ROC-AUC around `0.62` after 10 moves is realistic without Stockfish or deep learning. It is useful for:

- Ranking games by White-win likelihood.
- Building a baseline for future engine-enhanced research.
- Quantifying how much signal is gained from early board and clock state.

It is not strong enough for:

- Engine-level evaluation.
- Move recommendation.
- High-stakes betting or trading decisions without further calibration and robustness checks.

The Elo model is practically useful for same-stream repeat-player rating reconstruction. It should be paired with repeat/unseen diagnostics before being used in a cold-start setting.

## 13. Leakage And Reproducibility Assessment

The final pipeline is leakage-safe under the project assumptions:

- No validation rows are used for fitting.
- No result, termination, rating-diff, total length, or future-move features are used.
- Elo regression excludes current Elo and Elo-derived features.
- Causal player history is computed before current-game history updates.
- Clock features are limited to observed clocks inside the allowed horizon.
- No raw PGN or compressed database files are included in the submission package.

The final results are reproducible from the public Lichess database and the submitted code.
